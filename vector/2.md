# Embeddings Encode Meaning

## 0. What Is An Embedding?
An embedding is just **text turned into a list of numbers (a vector)** that represents its meaning.

**Simple words:** it's like giving every word or sentence a "meaning fingerprint" made of numbers, so a computer can compare meanings using math instead of matching exact words.

**Interview answer:** "An embedding is a numerical vector representation of text (or other data) such that semantically similar inputs produce vectors that are close together in vector space — unlike keyword matching or one-hot encoding, which only capture exact word overlap, not meaning."

## 1. king - man + woman ≈ queen
Embeddings don't just group similar words — they capture **relationships** as directions.

- king = royalty + male
- queen = royalty + female
- "man → woman" is a consistent **gender direction** in vector space

So: take king, remove "male" (subtract man), add "female" (add woman) → you land near queen.
Same direction also works for actor→actress, waiter→waitress — because it's one reusable direction, not a one-off coincidence.

**Simple words:** move in the "woman direction" from king, keep the royalty part → you get queen.

**Interview answer:** "Embeddings encode relationships between words as directions in vector space, not just similarity. Vector arithmetic like king - man + woman ≈ queen shows the model learned gender as a consistent, reusable direction — proof that embeddings capture meaning, not just word co-occurrence."

## 2. Embedding Models vs LLMs
Both are neural networks, but trained for different jobs.

| | Embedding model | LLM |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | output a vector for meaning | predict the next token |
| Training objective | similarity (contrastive) — pull similar meaning close, push different meaning apart | generation — guess the next word |
| Output | a fixed-size vector | text |

**Simple words:** an embedding model's job is to turn text into a "meaning fingerprint." An LLM's job is to keep writing the next word.

**Interview answer:** "An embedding model is trained with a contrastive objective — similar text pairs are pulled closer, dissimilar pairs pushed apart — producing a fixed vector representing meaning. An LLM is trained on next-token prediction. You can't reuse an LLM as an embedding model without different training, because the objectives are different."

## 3. Dimensions Trade-off (384 / 768 / 1536 / 3072)
More dimensions = more nuance captured, but more storage + slower search.
Fewer dimensions = faster and cheaper, but less precise.

**Simple words:** bigger vector = more detail about meaning, but heavier to store and compare.

**Interview answer:** "Embedding dimension is a trade-off between quality, storage cost, and search speed. 768–1536 is a common production sweet spot — enough nuance without excessive cost at scale."

## 4. Why Similar Text Gets Similar Vectors (Contrastive Learning)
Embedding models are trained on **pairs** — similar pairs (e.g. a question and its correct answer) are pushed close together in vector space, and dissimilar pairs are pushed apart. Millions of these examples teach the model which "directions" in space represent real meaning differences vs. which don't matter.

**Simple words:** the model practices on tons of "these two mean the same" / "these two don't" examples until similar meaning naturally lands close together.

**Interview answer:** "Similar text gets similar vectors because embedding models are trained with contrastive learning — pulling semantically similar pairs together and pushing dissimilar pairs apart across huge datasets, until proximity in vector space reliably reflects meaning."

## 5. Sentence vs Word Embeddings — Why We Embed Chunks in RAG
- Word embeddings: one vector per word (older approach, ignores context/order)
- Sentence/chunk embeddings: one vector for a whole passage, capturing context

In RAG, we embed **chunks** (paragraphs, not single words or whole documents) because:
- a whole document is too broad — averages out the meaning, loses precision
- single words lose context and relationships
- a chunk (a few sentences/paragraph) is the sweet spot — specific enough to match a query, big enough to contain full context

**Simple words:** we embed "chunks" of text (not words, not whole books) because that's the size where meaning is both precise and complete.

**Interview answer:** "We embed chunks rather than whole documents or single words because chunk-sized text balances specificity and context — small enough to retrieve precisely relevant content, large enough to preserve the surrounding meaning needed to answer a question."
